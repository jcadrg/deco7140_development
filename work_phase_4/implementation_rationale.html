Implementation Rationale

Alignment with Client and User Needs

The implementation phase expanded significantly on the original niche concept of a hobby-sharing platform. Initial ideas were oriented toward project galleries and passive browsing. However, based on feedback gathered through persona development (e.g. the retired crafter, the bookstagrammer mom, and the urban drone racer), the design evolved to support more dynamic, inclusive, and community-driven use cases. These personas emphasized the need for encouragement, emotional safety, and easy interaction—especially for users with varied digital confidence levels or limited time availability.

As implementation progressed, however, the target audience underwent a major shift. Reflecting insights from peer testing and iterative design reflection (Reflection Notebook, pp. 3–6), the platform transitioned toward a more focused demographic: tech-savvy DIY creators interested in structured learning, showcasing, and collaboration. This pivot was driven by feasibility concerns and the recognition that a more technically engaged audience would better suit the scope and capabilities of the project. Consequently, original personas were retired, and features were redesigned to support maker-oriented use cases—such as circuits tutorials, 3D printing workflows, and project-based learning.

These shifts directly align with the Connect Communities Initiative (CCI), which prioritizes creativity, belonging, and equitable access across digital experiences. For instance, the homepage was rewritten using plain, inclusive language that promotes a sense of belonging rather than achievement pressure (e.g. “where your passions belong” instead of “where your passions matter”). This follows WCAG 3.1.1 for readability and aligns with inclusive design recommendations from the Australian Government Style Manual and CCI guidelines.

Structurally, the site now supports multiple pathways for user interaction—such as project creation, group discovery, and profile personalization—based on research indicating users prefer platforms that support both expression and exploration (Reflection Notebook, pp. 3–6). These features also reflect CCI’s emphasis on low-pressure participation, ensuring that even new or less confident users feel empowered to contribute.

Accessibility and Ethical Considerations

Accessibility principles guided every aspect of the platform’s development. This included the use of semantic HTML5 elements (e.g. <section>, <article>, <nav>), visible focus indicators for keyboard navigation, and responsive feedback mechanisms on form inputs. All colors were chosen to meet WCAG 2.1 AA contrast ratios, particularly the recurring purple and pink tones used for calls to action and headers (WCAG 1.4.3). Typography and spacing were defined using rem units and CSS variables to ensure scalable legibility across devices.

Ethical considerations were also embedded throughout the design process. These included avoiding language that could stereotype users based on gender, age, or ability; designing forms without intrusive data collection; and ensuring users could browse and contribute without mandatory login or exposure to algorithmic ranking. The inclusive writing style was modeled on the Australian Government Style Manual and prioritized warmth, clarity, and neutrality.

These principles reflect the values of the Connect Communities Initiative (CCI), which encourages inclusive digital design as a pathway to creative empowerment. The team regularly cross-checked the interface against WCAG criteria using tools like IBM Equal Access Checker and followed internal checklists during development sprints to verify ethical and accessibility alignment (Reflection Notebook, p. 4–5).

Variations from A3 Design Proposal

Several key deviations from the A3 design proposal emerged during implementation, reflecting a shift in project scope, target audience, and interaction model. Initially envisioned as a general-purpose hobby-sharing platform, the site pivoted to focus on a tech-savvy DIY audience after deeper persona analysis and peer feedback (Reflection Notebook, p. 3–6). This shift resulted in discarding the original personas (e.g., retired crafter, bookstagrammer mom) in favor of new ones such as electronics hobbyists, 3D printing enthusiasts, and open-source hardware creators.

These changes impacted not only the visual language of the site—introducing bolder UI elements, streamlined navigation, and more technical vocabulary—but also structural decisions such as collapsing redundant categories (e.g. merging “Projects” and “Tutorials” into a unified card-based feed). The homepage was revised to promote exploration, rather than showcasing pre-set categories or curated highlights, based on insights suggesting that tech creators value discovery and peer-driven visibility.

Several UI components proposed in A3 (such as animated onboarding sequences and hover-state tooltips) were either removed or simplified due to implementation feasibility and timeline constraints. Similarly, some visual elements were adapted or replaced—such as the change in button size and layout hierarchy—to improve clarity and align with modular CSS practices introduced later in development.

Despite these adjustments, the final product remains aligned with the goals and values outlined in A3. Each change was supported by peer review, user observation, or feasibility assessment. The iterative design process was anchored by the values of the Connect Communities Initiative, particularly around reducing participation barriers and promoting sustainable, expressive tech-focused communities.

Challenges and Strategies

The implementation phase posed several technical and design challenges that required adaptive strategies and iterative problem-solving. One major issue was handling dynamic data parsing for user content. Originally, project and creator information were hardcoded, but to improve maintainability and simulate real-world functionality, the site was restructured to pull this data from modular JavaScript files (e.g., filters.js, details.js). This not only reduced redundancy but also laid the foundation for scalability in a potential live environment.

On the front-end, layout inconsistencies across devices were discovered late in development, particularly in margin spacing and component alignment. These were resolved using CSS variables and flexbox-based layout refinements that ensure responsiveness while preserving aesthetic consistency. Components such as the floating detail card were made mobile-friendly through viewport-aware positioning and scaling.

Ethically, tone and inclusion presented an ongoing concern. Several text blocks and prompts were revised to remove jargon, gendered language, or anything that could be interpreted as gatekeeping or exclusionary.

Overall, challenges were met with modular, scalable solutions that balanced time constraints with design integrity. Continuous peer feedback, reflection logs, and audit tools (e.g., Equal Access Checker) played a critical role in ensuring progress remained aligned with the platform’s inclusive and user-focused goals.

Form Functionality Instructions

The “My Hub” page includes working implementations of both GET and POST methods to interact with the community API.

When a user adds or edits their profile information, a POST request is triggered to submit the updated data to the backend endpoint. These actions occur through form submission buttons tied to authenticated users.

Additionally, a GET request is automatically performed every time the page loads or refreshes. This ensures that the most recent data is fetched and displayed immediately when the user visits their profile dashboard.

All data operations were tested to ensure correct field bindings, data persistence, and alignment with the expected API schema.
